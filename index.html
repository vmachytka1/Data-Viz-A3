Overarching Question

I would like to answer the following quesiton: How has the population distribution at the tails (i.e. very young, defined as 0-4 years, and very old, defined as 85+ years) evolved from 1900 to 2000, and what are the differences in these changes between genders?

My understanding is that significant advancements in public health and medical care impacted life expectancy and mortality rates. I would expect high infant mortality rates and relatively short life expectancies due to infectious diseases in the early 1900s. With improvements in sanitation and vaccines, more individuals reached advanced age. As a result, by the year 2000, a larger proportion of the population was able to live into their 80s and beyond. I would also be curious to observe how societal shifts have impacted family sizes and measure what proportion of the population consists of young individuals in 2000 vs in 1900. I am guessing that these trends will likely manifest differently across genders, especially given the differences in health outcomes and life expectancy between males and females. My goal for this exercise will be to visualize these shifts to better understand the reshaped distribution of age and gender structure of the American population.

Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Design Rationale for Sketch 1: For my first sketch, I decided to utilize a radial chart to depict the age groups in 1900 and 2000, allowing for a side-by-side comparison of the distributions. Each segment’s length corresponds to the population size of the respective age group and the color signifies the gender ratio within it. This way, we can directly compare how have age and gender distributions shifted over the last 100 years. This method will allow me to see the varying proportions of gender and how these have shifted over time. Moreover, I made the artistic choice to utilize the radial format to symbolize the lifecycle.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2: In my second sketch, I chose the stick-figure representations to humanize the data and to provide a more tangible sense of the population scale. Each figure corresponds to a specific number of individuals of a specific gender, and their placement on the age group axis allows for an easy comparison of growth and decline. Unlike the radial chart, which emphasized the proportion of gender within each age group, this visualization highlights the absolute population numbers by gender. I chose this style of visualization for its ability to communicate quantitative information and raw numbers easily.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3: For my third sketch, the two stacked bar charts connect the age groups from 1900 to 2000 and illustrate the growth or decrease of each demographic group. The use of lines with varying thickness indicates the magnitude of change and I selected a color for each group for easier comparison. I selected this visualization because it will emphasize the dynamic shifts between groups and what proportion of the population they contain.

Reflection

Each sketch highlights different insights: the radial chart captures the distribution of ages and the proportional changes of age and gender within it, the stick figures convey the absolute changes in population, and the stacked bar illustrates the demographic shift between individual age groups. However, a notable limitation across all sketches was the binary representation of gender. In future iterations of this project, a more inclusive approach could involve a reimagined way of classifying our data that acknowledges non-binary and other gender identities. This could then add further layers or new graphical elements into our future visualizations that better represent the of gender identities.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

I chose to explore the same question as in my sketches above: "How has the population distribution at the tails evolved from 1900 to 2000, and what are the differences in these changes between genders?" To understand the distribution of population, specifically the very young (0-4 years) and the very old (85+ years) from 1900 to 2000, I designed a visualization in Tableau that highlights the shifts in these age groups as well as the gender proportions.

I chose a dual population bar chart, which presents the data in both years side by side for direct comparison. My motivation for this format was clarity in spotting the relative sizes of total populations, as well as by specific age groups. I selected my colors to enhance the differentiation between male and female populations and represented each age group with stacked bars to easily compare the gender distribution within each group. I also made the analytical choice to compute gender percentages within each bucket to better understand the demographic shifts.

Through this design, I saw that there has been a significant shift in the population distribution at both the younger and older tails from 1900 to 2000. For the youngest age group, 0-4 years, the chart suggests a relative decrease in proportion to the total population by 2000, which could be indicative of lower birth rates or smaller family sizes as compared to 1900. On the other end of the spectrum, the marked increase in the population of those aged 85 and older in 2000 highlights a substantial rise in life expectancy, likely due to advancements in healthcare and overall improvements in quality of life. These changes at the tails of the age spectrum underscore the demographic transition experienced over the century and reveal pivotal shifts in societal structure and health outcomes.

Reflection

The process of arriving at this final design was inspired by my initial sketches from phase one. The radial chart's inspired the emphasis on relative size in the final visualization. The stick-figure sketch influenced the decision to include absolute population numbers. The third sketch, with its dynamic depiction of demographic shifts, contributed to the emphasis on visualizing the change over time. Sketching was very valuable for my final design: I was able to creatively explore various designs without the constraints of software tools. I explored various aspects of the data I normally wouldn’t have, such as the contrast in gender distribution across the age groups. The iterative process of moving from the sketches to Tableau solidified my understanding of the data and helped me refine my storytelling approach.
